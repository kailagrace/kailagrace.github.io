Read more about my work at Tyler.

During my internship in Maine for the summer of 2014, I worked as a UX intern in the financials division of Tyler Technologies, a company that creates software for the public sector. Though I cannot show direct examples of my work, I can do my best to explain it.

The main goal for me was to be involved in design, UX, and development. In UX specifically, I wanted to test out several different methods and approaches- Agile UX, Lean UX, and the Traditional UX (where the result is thorough documentation). My work spanned several projects and teams, but I managed to experience all of the above.

During my summer, I redesigned nine financial programs for maximum efficiency, using a new style pattern that had been tested. Two of these programs were developed and released during my time there. For these two programs, I also created HTML5/CSS3 prototypes to hand off to the development teams and speed the process of release.

Another project I worked on was assisting my mentor with creating an online webpage resource for developers. In order to create consistency across sectors and ease the development process, my mentor and I focused on creating an online webpage (much like getbootstrap.com) that would reflect all the styles used by the company. It showed each style in use, and gave directions through the content. The website also reflected these patterns through the source code, with the idea that it could be accessed and used.

I, along with another intern, also designed and created a completely new program to be incorporated into Tyler's software and used in town councils across the United States. I also got hands-on experience with the Lean UX process. Using this process of quick and recyclable wireframing, I tested one financial program and iterated until I came up with a final suggested design. This program is currently in development.

Human Factors Design Presentation

User-Experience Overhaul for University of Connecticut's Academic Student Services

For a final project in my Human Factors Engineering course, I met with the leading staff in UConn's Academic Student Services Center and agreed to assist them with a complete overhaul of their online website and their marketing strategy. In order to do this, I interviewed and surveyed students on campus to find out the answer to two main questions: 1. Whether they were aware of the ASC and the services that the center provides, and 2. Whether they had utilized the website. Using this information and that provided by the ASC, I created an individual Human Factors Analysis of their system, noting areas for improvement. Once complete, I brought in seven other students for User-Experience testing in order to test the usefulness and efficiency of the ASC's site. I tested their ability to achieve certain goals using the new sitemap and the old one, and recorded transcripts of our conversations, where I garnered information about their interactions with the site, both positive and negative. This unveiled other aspects of the department's website and marketing that were not reaching their full potential.

Given this data and information gathered, I established a renewed plan for the ASC in terms of social media and print marketing on campus, and restructured their sitemap to make their site easier for students to use.
